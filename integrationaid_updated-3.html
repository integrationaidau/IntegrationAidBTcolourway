The science of how they learn

Our tutors don't guess. Every strategy we use is grounded in peer-reviewed research into how neurodivergent brains process, retain, and thrive. Explore the evidence behind our approach.

ADHD & Executive Function

The ADHD Brain: Differently Wired, Not Deficient

ADHD involves genuine neurological differences in dopamine and norepinephrine regulation, affecting the prefrontal cortex — the region responsible for planning, impulse control, and sustained attention. Understanding this changes everything about how we teach.

3–5×
Higher task engagement when intrinsic interest is activated in ADHD learners
30%
Estimated developmental lag in executive function relative to neurotypical peers
2×
Better recall when physical movement is incorporated into learning activities

Variable Attention Windows

Research by Barkley (2015) shows ADHD learners don't lack attention — they lack the ability to direct attention on demand. When interest is high (hyperfocus), attention can be extraordinary. Our tutors identify and leverage each child's interest anchors to make concepts click.

Working Memory Challenges

Studies in neuroscience confirm ADHD is associated with reduced working memory capacity. This means multi-step instructions often fail before step two. We break tasks into single micro-steps, use visual anchors, and never rely on verbal instruction alone.

Dopamine & Motivation

ADHD brains have lower baseline dopamine availability. Routine, repetitive tasks fail to generate enough dopamine for engagement. We use novelty, game-based elements, and immediate positive feedback loops to naturally boost dopamine during sessions.

Emotional Dysregulation

ADHD frequently co-occurs with emotional dysregulation. Frustration tolerance is lower, and rejection-sensitive dysphoria is common. Our tutors are trained to de-escalate before it reaches a tipping point, using calm, non-punitive redirection strategies.

How IntegrationAid applies this research

1
Time-chunked sessions

We use the Pomodoro principle adapted for children — short bursts of focused work (8–12 mins) with movement or sensory breaks built in, aligned with research on ADHD attention windows.

2
Interest-anchored curriculum

Before we begin, we interview families to understand your child's passions. We then embed curriculum content inside those interests — maths through sport statistics, literacy through gaming narratives.

3
Visual task scaffolding

All multi-step tasks are presented visually, never just spoken. We use colour-coded steps, progress trackers, and physical checklists to externalise working memory and reduce cognitive load.

4
Immediate, specific feedback

Vague praise doesn't activate the dopamine system. We give immediate, specific positive feedback ("That was a brilliant strategy for carrying the tens") to create the neurochemical reward loop that sustains engagement.

"Children with ADHD are not broken versions of neurotypical children. They are differently wired, and when education meets them where they are, their potential is extraordinary."

— Dr. Russell Barkley, ADHD Neuroscience Researcher

Autism Spectrum Disorder

The Autistic Brain: A Different Architecture of Thought

Autism involves differences in neural connectivity — particularly in social cognition networks and sensory processing pathways. Autistic learners often exhibit heightened pattern recognition, deep focus on areas of interest, and a preference for explicit, predictable structure. Our approach honours all of this.

76%
Of autistic students show superior performance in structured, predictable environments
40%
Of autistic individuals show exceptional ability in at least one cognitive domain
2×
More effective knowledge retention when visual supports accompany verbal instruction

Predictability & Routine

Autistic brains are highly attuned to pattern and predictability. Unexpected changes cause genuine neurological stress. Our sessions follow the same structure each time — same opening, same flow, same closing — so every session is a known quantity before it begins.

Explicit Social & Communication Instruction

Social inference — reading between the lines — doesn't come naturally to many autistic learners. We never expect children to "pick up" implicit rules. We make every expectation explicit, written, and concrete, reducing the cognitive energy spent on ambiguity.

Special Interests as Learning Vehicles

Research consistently shows that embedding academic content within a child's special interest produces dramatically better engagement and retention. An autistic child obsessed with trains can learn fractions through timetables, geography through rail maps, and history through the evolution of railways.

Processing Time & Reduced Demands

Autistic learners often need more processing time before responding. Rushing to fill silence creates anxiety and shuts down learning. Our tutors are trained to wait — genuinely — and never interpret a pause as non-understanding.

How IntegrationAid applies this research

1
Consistent session structure

Every session begins with a written visual agenda. No surprises. The child sees exactly what's happening, in what order, and for how long — before we start.

2
Special interest integration

We spend the first session (and ongoing check-ins) mapping your child's interests. Curriculum content is then re-framed through those lenses — making abstract concepts tangible and personally meaningful.

3
Explicit instruction only

We never say "figure it out." Every step, every expectation, every transition is stated clearly and confirmed. We don't reward reading between the lines — we remove the need to do so entirely.

4
Sensory & communication preferences honoured

We ask families upfront: does your child prefer minimal eye contact? Typed communication? Fidget tools? We build these into the session design from day one.

"The most effective interventions for autistic learners are those that work with the brain's architecture rather than against it — predictability, interest-based learning, and explicit instruction are not accommodations, they are best practice."

— Professor Tony Attwood, Autism Specialist

Every strategy above is built into how our tutors work from session one.

Dyslexia & Dysgraphia

Dyslexia: A Phonological Processing Difference

Dyslexia is not about seeing letters backwards. It is a neurological difference in the phonological processing system — the brain's ability to link sounds to symbols. Dysgraphia involves motor-planning differences that affect writing. Both are highly responsive to structured, multi-sensory teaching.

95%
Of dyslexic students respond positively to structured literacy programs with multi-sensory methods
1 in 5
Students have dyslexia, making it the most common learning difference in Australian classrooms
IQ
Dyslexia has no correlation with intelligence — many highly gifted individuals are dyslexic

The Phonological Deficit

Neuroimaging research (Shaywitz, 2003) shows dyslexic readers use different brain pathways for reading — relying more on slower, less efficient routes. Structured literacy (Orton-Gillingham principles) actively builds the efficient phonological pathway through repetition and multi-sensory input.

Dysgraphia & Motor Planning

Dysgraphia involves difficulty with the motor sequencing required for handwriting. The brain's motor planning centres struggle to automate letter formation. We use a combination of keyboarding, speech-to-text, and structured handwriting practice to build fluency across multiple output channels.

Compensatory Strengths

Dyslexic learners often demonstrate superior skills in spatial reasoning, big-picture thinking, storytelling, and creative problem-solving. Research by West (1991) links dyslexia with success in fields requiring 3D visualisation. We build on these strengths, never just around the deficit.

Decoding vs Comprehension

Many dyslexic readers have excellent comprehension when text is read aloud — the barrier is decoding, not understanding. We use audiobooks, read-alouds, and text-to-speech alongside decoding practice, so children can demonstrate comprehension while building fluency in parallel.

How IntegrationAid applies this research

1
Orton-Gillingham informed instruction

Our tutors use structured, systematic, multi-sensory literacy teaching — tracing letters while saying sounds, tapping out syllables physically, and colour-coding phoneme patterns.

2
Phonemic awareness before phonics

We start with sound awareness — can your child hear the difference between /d/ and /t/? — before introducing the symbol-sound link. This mirrors the brain's natural literacy acquisition sequence.

3
Multiple output channels

We never limit expression to handwriting. Children demonstrate understanding through speech, drawing, typing, or verbal discussion — whichever channel removes the writing bottleneck so thinking can shine through.

4
Strength-led sessions

Every session explicitly celebrates what your child does brilliantly — their creativity, their spatial intelligence, their verbal storytelling. Building identity as a capable learner is as important as building decoding skills.

"Dyslexia is not a sign of poor intelligence or laziness. It is a different kind of mind, and given the right instruction, dyslexic learners can achieve remarkable literacy outcomes."

— Dr. Sally Shaywitz, Yale Center for Dyslexia & Creativity

Supporting your child with dyslexia?

Our structured literacy approach is evidence-based and immediately effective.

Dyscalculia

Dyscalculia: A Number Sense Processing Difference

Dyscalculia is a specific learning difficulty affecting mathematical processing — particularly number sense, magnitude comparison, and arithmetic fact retrieval. It is as common as dyslexia but far less understood. With the right concrete, visual approach, mathematical confidence is absolutely achievable.

6%
Of the population has dyscalculia — comparable in prevalence to dyslexia
Concrete
Physical manipulation of objects is the most effective first stage of numeracy instruction
50%+
Of students with dyscalculia also experience maths anxiety as a secondary challenge

The Number Sense Deficit

Research by Butterworth (2005) shows dyscalculic learners have difficulty with the intuitive sense of quantity — the ability to immediately perceive that 7 is more than 5. This foundational gap cascades into difficulty with all higher maths. We start at the foundation, always.

Concrete-Pictorial-Abstract (CPA)

Singapore Mathematics research establishes that all learners — especially those with dyscalculia — must move through concrete (physical objects), pictorial (diagrams), then abstract (symbols) stages before number facts become secure. We never skip stages to "save time."

Maths Anxiety & the Brain

FMRI research shows maths anxiety activates the brain's pain and threat centres — the same regions activated by physical danger. This shuts down the prefrontal cortex needed for maths reasoning. Reducing anxiety isn't soft — it's a neurological prerequisite for mathematical learning.

Verbal vs Visual Pathways

Neurotypical learners often retrieve maths facts via verbal memory (reciting times tables). Dyscalculic learners have weaker access to this pathway. We build alternative visual-spatial routes to the same facts — grid models, dot patterns, area representations — that bypass the deficit.

How IntegrationAid applies this research

1
Physical manipulatives first

We use blocks, counters, number lines, and physical objects before any written number work. Quantity must be felt before it can be symbolised.

2
No rote memorisation pressure

We never drill times tables in isolation. We build multiplicative reasoning through patterns, arrays, and skip-counting so facts emerge from understanding, not anxiety-inducing repetition.

3
Anxiety reduction as priority

The first sessions focus on establishing that maths is safe here. We celebrate process over answer, normalise mistakes, and never introduce time pressure until confidence is established.

4
Visual-spatial number representations

We use ten-frames, base-10 blocks, area models, and bar diagrams to give children multiple visual representations of every number concept — building the alternative brain pathways the research identifies.

"Dyscalculia is not about being 'bad at maths.' It is a specific difficulty with number sense that, when addressed with the right concrete and visual approach, responds remarkably well to intervention."

— Professor Brian Butterworth, UCL Institute of Cognitive Neuroscience

🌿 Anxiety & School Avoidance

Anxiety in Learning: When the Brain is Stuck in Threat Mode

Anxiety activates the brain's amygdala — the threat detection system — which physically inhibits the prefrontal cortex, the region needed for learning. A child who is anxious cannot learn effectively, regardless of their intelligence. Safety must come before curriculum.

1 in 7
Australian children experience clinically significant anxiety — many undiagnosed
Amygdala
Hijack: anxiety activates the threat brain, blocking the learning brain from functioning
Predictability
Is the single most effective environmental intervention for reducing school anxiety

The Polyvagal Window of Tolerance

Polyvagal theory (Porges, 2011) shows that children can only access higher-order learning when their nervous system is within a "window of tolerance" — neither hyperaroused (panic) nor hypoaroused (shutdown). Our sessions are designed to keep children regulated and within this window throughout.

Attachment & Felt Safety

Research by Perry (2006) shows the brain's capacity to learn is directly proportional to the child's sense of felt safety. Building a warm, trusting tutor-student relationship is not optional — it is the neurological foundation upon which all academic work is built.

School Refusal & Avoidance

School avoidance is a trauma response, not a behavioural choice. Forcing attendance without addressing the underlying anxiety typically worsens the trajectory. Our tutors are trained in gradual exposure principles — building safety in learning contexts before gradually increasing demands.

Perfectionism & Academic Anxiety

Many high-achieving neurodivergent children develop academic anxiety through perfectionism — the fear of getting it wrong becomes paralysing. We explicitly teach that mistakes are information, model making errors ourselves, and structure sessions so effort is celebrated over outcome.

How IntegrationAid applies this research

1
Relationship first, curriculum second

The first sessions are entirely about building trust and felt safety. We don't push academic content until the child is genuinely comfortable. This investment pays compounding returns.

2
Predictable, low-demand entry

Every session begins with a low-stakes, high-success activity — something the child finds easy and enjoyable. This regulates the nervous system and opens the learning brain before harder tasks begin.

3
No time pressure, ever

Timers, speed tests, and competitive activities are removed from our sessions. Chronic time pressure is a significant anxiety trigger that contributes nothing to genuine learning.

4
Gradual challenge scaling

We use a deliberate "barely above comfortable" principle — tasks are just challenging enough to grow, but never so hard they activate the threat system. This is the zone of proximal development, applied precisely.

"You cannot educate a child who doesn't feel safe. The prerequisite for learning is regulation, and the prerequisite for regulation is relationship."

— Dr. Bruce Perry, Child Trauma Neuroscientist

🌊 Sensory Processing

Sensory Processing: When the World is Too Loud, Too Bright, Too Much

Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD) involves atypical modulation of sensory input — some children are hypersensitive (overwhelmed by ordinary sensation) and others are hyposensitive (seeking intense sensory input). Both profiles significantly affect the ability to learn in standard classroom environments. Our sessions are designed around sensory needs from the ground up.

80%
Of autistic individuals also have significant sensory processing differences
Sensory
Diet: personalised sensory input schedules significantly improve focus and regulation
5–10%
Of children without other diagnoses are estimated to have clinically significant SPD

Hyper & Hyposensitivity

Research distinguishes between over-responsive (hypersensitive) children — who find fluorescent lights, background noise, or the texture of materials genuinely distressing — and under-responsive (hyposensitive) children who seek intense input to feel regulated. We profile each child and design sessions accordingly.

The Vestibular & Proprioceptive Systems

Beyond the five classic senses, two internal systems profoundly affect learning: the vestibular system (balance and movement) and proprioception (body awareness). Children who fidget, rock, or seek physical pressure are often self-regulating through these systems. We build movement into sessions rather than suppressing it.

Sensory Diet & Regulation

Occupational therapy research supports "sensory diets" — personalised schedules of sensory input that help children maintain optimal arousal for learning. We collaborate with families and therapists to understand and replicate effective sensory supports during sessions.

Environmental Modification

Classrooms are often sensory hostile — fluorescent lights, unpredictable noise, crowding, synthetic materials. Our sessions are conducted in calm, predictable, low-stimulation environments (or online), with materials chosen to avoid known sensory triggers and include preferred sensory inputs.

How IntegrationAid applies this research

1
Sensory profile intake

Before sessions begin, we gather detailed information about your child's sensory profile — what overwhelms them, what regulates them, what textures and sounds to avoid, and what sensory inputs help them focus.

2
Movement breaks by design

Physical movement — jumping, heavy work, proprioceptive input — is built into every session as a regulatory tool, not a reward or an afterthought. We time breaks to the child's regulation needs, not a clock.

3
Sensory-friendly materials

We select worksheets, manipulatives, and resources based on sensory profile — avoiding glossy paper that causes glare, scratchy textures, or visually overwhelming layouts. Materials are chosen as deliberately as content.

4
Therapist report integration

We actively request and read OT reports and sensory profiles. Our sessions are designed to extend and reinforce the regulatory strategies your child's therapist has recommended — not work in isolation from them.

"Sensory processing differences are not behavioural choices. When we modify the environment to meet the child rather than forcing the child to endure a hostile environment, learning becomes possible."

— Dr. Lucy Jane Miller, SPD Foundation
